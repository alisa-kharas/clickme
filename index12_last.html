A book about a book
Book remake
This small book (45x63 mm) is a remake of 1933 book about physics
The original book looks like being handwritten even though its edition is 3000
It reminded me of a cheat sheet so I started looking for the ways the cheat sheet can be made, where and what features it has
It is usually handwritten, messy, tiny and might even be put on skin, clothes or small folded paper
I tried to make it even smaller than a pocket-size but still readable and easy to read through
As it’s a printed object, I focused a lot on paper
For the paper, at first, I was looking for 90 gsm and 150 gsm paper but stayed with 90gsm as its the thinnest possible
I looked at Irma Boom: Biography in Books This miniature book (1.5 X 2 inches) contains a complete overview of her work, with commentary and more than 450 full-color illustrations in 704 pages with printed edges. This little book consists of plenty of pages but is still small and readable. Irma uses very thin paper for her book

How to put on shoes
Instruction
I decided to choose a very basic action such as putting on shoes
I found the problems a person might face while making this action
The issues were identifying the correct shoe for each leg
And the direction of putting your foot inside
Shoe identity was solved by making the outline for each shoe
So that a user will be able to compare the boot curves with the drawn curves
And
The proper direction of sticking your foot inside was solved by making the arrow that shows the way
Moreover
I tried to simplify the steps in the manual in order to make it easier to follow them and so they would fit on a small door mat and still would be readable
Also
My main focus in this project was the surface the action takes place
The door mat is the most common place you put on shoes so I find it the most convenient surface to put on instruction
I started my research with primary by putting my shoes on myself and watching youtube video instructions
That helped me to realise the actions I make, in which order and how I actually make them
And I realised that the instruction needs to be universal so there is no difference which type of shoes you put on
I read the article about teaching a kid how to put on shoes helped me most as I finally realised that action we all do automatically actually might have some issues such as which shoe is right and which is left for example
I researched different instructions as well, but from my personal experience the combination of image and text the most successful

blah blah blah
Typographical transcript
I was working with a lecture by Sheila Levrant De Bretteville at Walkers Art centre about her career and her projects
Specifically with a 3 minute part of her conference where she mostly talks about her one of the most recognisable feminist sign – eye bolt
I picked this part of her speech as she talks passionately about this particular project and because of that has some high voice parts and quite a lot of slips of the tongue
I analysed her speech from the sound, trying to notice her speech features
She does have an American accent, but it doesn’t affect her talking a lot
Occasionally her voice level does change and noticeably her voice emphases just a part of the word
Typographically I illustrated that with capital letters making the rest of the text low case letter
Secondly,
Her other speech feature is her tongue slips
So she says a word or a couple of them and then corrects herself telling the right phrase
I showed it by at first writing the correct sentence with the same paragraph style as the rest of the text and then fitting her blooper and the correct phrase in the gap the size of the right words by decreasing letter spacing
Talking about the type choice
After the research on feminist fonts, I found that many feminist artists in their artworks used Futura typeface
So I decided to stay with it as it’s sans-serif which reflects Sheila’s design as well
For the paper
I made in-depth research on Sheila’s artworks and found out that she had nickname ‘Pinky’ because her recent very successful project was about the pink colour
Moreover, the pink colour stands for feminism and fits De Bretteville perfectly as she is an influential feminist activist. So my choice was uncoated 120 gsm pink paper

Miles Aldridge exhibition teaser for Lumiere centre of photography
I worked with ‘I only want you to love me’ photograph by Miles Aldridge
The pattern on the floor in that photograph was a perfect reference for a game–checkers
I started going more in-depth in this game and realised that there are always two opposers
So one can play with checkers that describe Aldridge’s work
And the opposer will have antonyms
I chose foam board for the background as I wanted to keep the vivid colour of the pattern and the best way to achieve that was the printer and right file preparation
Talking about checkers, I worked with plywood and laser-cut and then coloured them
I wanted checkers to be 3d and look like objects you can play with
And I colored checkers with spray paint as it gives most vibrant and smooth coverage
And foam will have a thickness so won’t look childish and very handmade
I decided to make a script for the game
Which will help me to control what’s in the shot
So I chose two pairs of checkers: vibrant colour–pale colour, movement–stillness as I find it most relevant to Miles Aldridge
When filming
I decided to choose red for the background to complete the vivid colour palette of Aldridge’s photo
